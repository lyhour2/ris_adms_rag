REGULATORY COMPLIANCE FRAMEWORK — GDPR AND EU AI ACT
Document ID: REG-COMP-2024
Last reviewed: 2024-01

PART A — GENERAL DATA PROTECTION REGULATION (GDPR)

Article 6(1)(b) — Legal Basis for Processing
Credit scoring and automated decision-making for loan origination is lawful under
Art.6(1)(b) GDPR as processing necessary for the performance of a contract to which
the data subject is party. All variables used in the scoring model must have documented
legal basis. Inferred or derived variables created from behavioural data require
explicit consent documentation under Art.9 where sensitive categories are involved.

Article 22 — Automated Individual Decision-Making
Credit decisions made solely by automated means have significant legal effect on
applicants. Under Art.22 GDPR, applicants have the right to:
(a) Obtain human intervention in the decision process
(b) Express their point of view
(c) Obtain an explanation of the decision
(d) Contest the decision

Compliance obligation: Every automated credit decision must be accompanied by a
meaningful explanation that references the specific data points used and their impact
on the outcome. This explanation must be available to the applicant on request and
must not consist solely of a score or a category.

Data minimisation (Art.5(1)(c)): Only data strictly necessary for creditworthiness
assessment should be processed. Use of social media data, location data, or
psychographic profiles is not permitted without specific regulatory authorisation.

PART B — EU ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE ACT (AI ACT)

Classification of Credit Scoring Systems
Credit scoring systems used for granting or denying access to financial services are
classified as HIGH-RISK AI systems under Annex III, point 5(b) of the EU AI Act.

Obligations for High-Risk AI Systems (Chapter 3):

Article 9 — Risk Management System
A continuous risk management process must be established and documented for the
AI system. This includes identification and analysis of known and foreseeable risks,
estimation of risks arising from intended use, and implementation of risk management
measures.

Article 13 — Transparency and Provision of Information
High-risk AI systems must be designed to ensure sufficient transparency to enable
deployers to interpret the system's output. Technical documentation must include
a description of the system's performance on relevant populations, including any
known or foreseeable limitations.

Article 14 — Human Oversight
High-risk AI systems must be designed to allow effective human oversight. Deployers
must assign the task of human oversight to natural persons with the necessary
competence, training, and authority to intervene. Automated rejections must be
reviewable by a natural person.

Article 17 — Quality Management System
Providers must implement a quality management system including policies and
procedures for data governance, technical documentation, record keeping, and
post-market monitoring.

Logging obligations: All inputs, outputs, and decisions of the AI system must be
logged with sufficient detail to enable post-hoc review. Logs must be retained
for a period of at least 10 years for credit decisions affecting consumers.
